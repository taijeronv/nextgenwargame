# CADE Showcase Website Plan

This plan turns the curated CADE source material into a website that showcases CADE as the capstone project and uses the project itself to demonstrate AI management/operator skill.

## Goal

Rebuild the homepage from a resume-style site into a CADE case-study site. The page should make visitors understand three things quickly:

- CADE is a real, validated training framework for combined arms staff decision-making under pressure.
- AI was used as a managed production system, not as a gimmick or autonomous replacement for design judgment.
- The strongest skill being demonstrated is AI operations: framing the problem, governing AI outputs, preserving source truth, building repeatable workflows, validating execution, and improving the product from evidence.

## Narrative Thesis

Primary thesis:

> CADE shows what competent AI management looks like in a high-consequence domain: human design authority, AI production speed, disciplined workflow governance, and execution evidence.

Supporting message:

> The result was not just a collection of AI-generated documents. It was a replicable training product framework with source truth, turn rhythm, adjudication, controller discipline, learning capture, and adoption standards.

## Audience

The site should work for three audience types:

- AI/product audience: wants to see how AI was operationalized into a real workflow.
- Military/training audience: wants to understand what CADE is, when it fits, and why it is credible.
- Hiring/collaboration audience: wants evidence that TJ can manage AI tools, domain constraints, and delivery under pressure.

## Positioning Rules

- Present CADE as a constrained-environment training framework, not a general-purpose exercise platform.
- Present AI as the production engine, not the design authority.
- Avoid overclaiming: CADE supplements, but does not replace, doctrinal certification or high-fidelity simulation.
- Use "Controller Package" consistently for the v3.0 runtime artifact; do not blur it with the legacy runbook.
- Emphasize judgment, governance, validation, and iteration as the AI management skill set.

## Proposed Page Structure

### 1. Hero: The Capstone

Purpose:

- Establish CADE as the central project.
- Lead with the human-plus-AI operating model.
- Make the page feel like a case study, not a resume.

Core content:

- CADE: Combined Arms Decision Exercise.
- "Human design authority. AI production speed. A replicable training framework."
- One-line explanation: a consequence-based staff decision exercise built using AI research tools, doctrine-grounded workflows, and a structured decision format.

AI skill to weave in:

- AI operator as system architect: used AI to accelerate research, drafting, artifact production, and iteration while preserving human governance.

Suggested UI:

- Large CADE title.
- Three proof chips: "AI-assisted build", "Controller Package v3.0", "Validated in live execution".
- Primary CTA: "See the system".
- Secondary CTA: "How AI was managed".

### 2. Why CADE Exists: The Constraint Problem

Purpose:

- Explain the real-world problem before explaining the solution.
- Show that the AI workflow was designed around constraints.

Core content:

- Compressed training time.
- Uneven doctrinal baselines.
- Mixed language proficiency.
- Commanders needed practical deliverables inside short event windows.
- Traditional methods were valid but often mismatched when setup time consumed training time.

AI skill to weave in:

- Problem framing: identifying where AI could reduce preparation overhead without weakening training value.

Suggested UI:

- Four friction-point cards.
- A "fit, not superiority" comparison showing CADE beside simulation/CPX/TDG as different tools for different objectives.

### 3. What CADE Trains: Decision Behavior Under Pressure

Purpose:

- Clarify the training target.
- Prevent the project from being mistaken for a doctrine quiz, slide deck, or simulation replacement.

Core content:

- CADE trains decision behavior, not doctrinal recall.
- Staffs must make explicit tradeoffs.
- Cross-functional integration becomes observable.
- Decisions persist and consequences compound.

AI skill to weave in:

- Translating ambiguous training goals into observable behavior markers that AI-generated materials could support.

Suggested UI:

- Short explanatory text plus four behavior markers.
- Include the boundary: not certification, not pass/fail grading, not a resettable sandbox.

### 4. How CADE Works: Turn Rhythm And Consequence Loop

Purpose:

- Make the exercise mechanics legible.
- Give visitors a concrete mental model.

Core content:

- Six-phase turn rhythm from the design brief: Situation, Clarification, Deliberation, Decision, Brief to CDR, Adjudication.
- PRD lifecycle variant to reconcile in copy: situation update, deliberation, decision commitment, adjudication, review.
- Persistent consequence loop: resource depletion, casualties, timing shifts, and option degradation carry forward.

AI skill to weave in:

- Workflow design: fixed sequence and artifact chain made AI outputs comparable, controllable, and useful.

Suggested UI:

- Process diagram or loop.
- Highlight "fixed rhythm makes the exercise assessable."

Content decision needed:

- Decide whether final public copy uses the six-phase briefing model or the five-phase PRD lifecycle. If both remain valid, present the six-phase model as the classroom turn sequence and the five-module lifecycle as product architecture.

### 5. What AI Did vs What I Governed

Purpose:

- Directly showcase AI management/operator skill.
- Make the page about capability, not just CADE content.

Core content:

- AI generated research, OPORD shell, draft artifacts, turn content, and revised controller materials.
- Human design authority identified the training gap, set success markers, authored the turn sequence, wrote adjudication logic, set controller standards, observed live execution, and approved redesign.

AI skill to weave in:

- Tool orchestration.
- Prompt/workflow governance.
- Source-truth discipline.
- Output review.
- Domain validation.
- Iterative refinement.

Suggested UI:

- Two-column comparison: "AI Production Engine" and "Human Design Authority".
- Add a center connector: "Managed workflow: requirements -> source truth -> drafts -> review -> package -> validation -> revision."

### 6. Product Architecture: CADE v3.0 Framework

Purpose:

- Show CADE has matured into a product framework.
- Replace generic project-card structure with a clear system architecture.

Core content:

- Source-governed scenario package.
- Turn engine.
- Adjudication model.
- Controller delivery package.
- Learning capture layer.

AI skill to weave in:

- Systems design: converting AI-generated content into modules with acceptance criteria and runtime controls.

Suggested UI:

- Five-module architecture diagram.
- Each module has "what it does" and "operator skill demonstrated."

Example module framing:

- Source Package: prevents AI drift by anchoring all artifacts to OPORD-quality source truth.
- Turn Engine: converts training intent into repeatable decision cycles.
- Adjudication Model: reduces controller variance through observable outcome bands.
- Controller Package: makes execution portable by consolidating runtime guidance.
- Learning Capture: turns exercise decisions into structured feedback without becoming a lecture.

### 7. Controller Package: The Scalability Move

Purpose:

- Highlight the most important product-design evolution.
- Show the difference between a designer-dependent prototype and a portable framework.

Core content:

- v2.4 centered on a runbook and fragmented supporting products.
- v3.0 centers on a Unified Controller Package.
- Controller Package consolidates execution logic, quick references, role aids, decision prompts, support tools, AAR structure, and role boundaries.
- Target: a controller can find required reference material within 60 seconds under pressure.

AI skill to weave in:

- Runtime usability: recognizing that good AI-generated content fails if humans cannot use it under time pressure.

Suggested UI:

- Before/after card: "Runbook-centered" vs "Controller Package-centered".
- Feature list for the package.
- Optional artifact preview area if sanitized screenshots become available.

### 8. Evidence: What Live Execution Proved

Purpose:

- Establish credibility through validation, not self-description.

Core content:

- January 2026 live execution criteria were set before execution.
- Staff operated inside the structure.
- Controllers executed with delivered artifacts.
- Consequences created meaningful decision pressure.
- Review captured learning before the next turn.
- Later evidence drove Controller Package consolidation, deterministic outcome bands, and protected AAR time boxes.

AI skill to weave in:

- Evidence-driven iteration: measuring failure modes and redesigning the system rather than polishing prose.

Suggested UI:

- Validation checklist.
- "Observed issue -> design response" table.

Content decision needed:

- Decide whether to name "January 29-30, 2026" publicly or use "live execution in January 2026."

### 9. How Success Is Measured

Purpose:

- Show rigor and product maturity.
- Make the case that CADE has operational measures, not vague impact claims.

Core content:

- Facilitator independence.
- Decision pressure.
- Adjudication consistency.
- Consequence persistence.
- AAR quality.
- Multilingual usability.
- Preparation overhead.

AI skill to weave in:

- Translating qualitative training effects into observable success metrics and thresholds.

Suggested UI:

- Metric cards.
- Use public-safe thresholds only after confirmation.

Content decision needed:

- Confirm whether to publish exact thresholds: 80% adjudication consistency, 60-second reference retrieval, 3-turn/2-day event.

### 10. Designed Against Failure Modes

Purpose:

- Demonstrate professional maturity and risk management.

Core content:

- Risk: controller discipline degrades. Control: role boundary documentation and rehearsal.
- Risk: adjudication inconsistency. Control: deterministic outcome bands and calibration.
- Risk: language friction. Control: visual-forward materials and plain-language phrasing.
- Risk: consequence tracking breaks down. Control: state tracking sheet and multi-turn rehearsal.
- Risk: scenario drift. Control: source-governed package rule.

AI skill to weave in:

- AI operations requires guardrails. The system anticipates where human/AI workflows fail and builds controls around those failure modes.

Suggested UI:

- "Risk -> Control" cards.

### 11. Where CADE Fits

Purpose:

- Preserve credibility by being explicit about best-fit and out-of-scope cases.

Core content:

- Best fit: compressed time, uneven staff proficiency, integration focus, decision behavior under pressure, language friction.
- Less appropriate: doctrinal certification, high-fidelity technical simulation as the primary goal, long-term individual skill development, open-ended sandboxing.

AI skill to weave in:

- Operator judgment: knowing where a tool fits is as important as knowing how to use it.

Suggested UI:

- "Use CADE when..." and "Use another method when..." comparison.

### 12. Closing: What This Demonstrates About My AI Skill

Purpose:

- Tie the case study back to the user's desired personal positioning.
- Make the hiring/collaboration signal explicit without reverting to a resume page.

Core content:

- I can turn vague requirements into a governed AI production workflow.
- I can keep AI outputs grounded in a source of truth.
- I can design repeatable artifacts for real users under pressure.
- I can test AI-supported products against live behavior.
- I can iterate from execution evidence.

Suggested UI:

- Concise closing statement.
- Contact CTA: "Talk about CADE, AI-assisted training design, or AI operations."

## Visual Direction

The visual style should feel like an operational command-and-control case study, not a startup SaaS page.

Recommended approach:

- Dark base remains acceptable, but make it more intentional with grid lines, map-like panels, signal accents, and structured cards.
- Use color sparingly: blue for AI/workflow, amber for decision pressure, green for validation/proof.
- Add process diagrams instead of more text cards.
- Use "artifact preview" panels for Controller Package, OPORD source truth, turn sequence, and AAR capture if sanitized examples are available.

Avoid:

- Generic "AI portfolio" language.
- Resume-first sections.
- Overuse of abstract icons without concrete proof.
- Claiming AI replaced expert judgment.

## Open Decisions

- Should public copy say "Ukrainian officer training" or use a generalized description?
- Should the hero include "two-person team" and "one week"?
- Should the site publish exact performance thresholds such as 80% adjudication consistency and 60-second reference retrieval?
- Should the page frame CADE as v3.0, v3.1, or simply "current CADE framework"?
- Which sanitized artifacts can be displayed?
- Should the site include a separate downloadable CADE overview PDF?
- Is the primary CTA "Talk about CADE" or "See how it was built"?

## Success Criteria For The Website

- A visitor can explain what CADE is within 30 seconds.
- A visitor can identify how AI was used and what remained human-governed.
- A visitor sees concrete proof: live validation, Controller Package evolution, metrics, or artifacts.
- The page demonstrates AI operator skill through the CADE story rather than through a generic skills list.
- The site avoids overclaiming and clearly states fit, boundaries, and governance.
