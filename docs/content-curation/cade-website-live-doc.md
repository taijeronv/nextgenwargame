### High-Value Website Positioning

- CADE is a battalion staff capstone exercise built by a two-person team in one week using AI research tools and a structured decision format.
- CADE proves high-fidelity capstone training does not require high-overhead simulation platforms.
- The core value proposition is professional staff training outcomes at lower setup cost through AI-assisted scenario development and a structured decision format.
- CADE is best framed as an AI-enabled training design system, not just a one-off exercise product.
- The strongest public-facing story is: human design authority plus AI production speed produced a replicable program.

### Problem Framing

CADE started from four friction points:

- Event time constrained both planning and execution.
- Doctrinal knowledge varied significantly across staff participants.
- Mixed language proficiency degraded instruction quality and coordination speed.
- Commanders required practical deliverables within compressed event windows.

Website relevance:

- This supports a "Why CADE Exists" section.
- It connects the project to real operational constraints rather than abstract AI experimentation.
- It explains why AI mattered: not because AI was novel, but because the training environment was constrained.

Potential section headline:

- "CADE was built for the constraints that break traditional training formats."

### Method Fit And Differentiation

The design brief positions CADE against JCATS, DXTRS, and CPX:

| Method | Strength | Constraint |
| --- | --- | --- |
| JCATS | High-fidelity simulation | Days to configure; trained operators required throughout |
| DXTRS | Planning-tool value | Same overhead dependencies as JCATS |
| CPX | Tests process breadth | Requires more time than available |
| CADE | Decision-quality under pressure | Designed to avoid those overhead constraints |

Website relevance:

- Use this carefully. It should not attack other systems; it should explain fit.
- CADE is not a universal replacement for simulation. It is a better fit when the objective is decision behavior under time pressure and setup time is constrained.

Potential section headline:

- "The problem was not the methods. It was fit."

### Training Purpose

CADE was designed to train decision behavior, not doctrinal recall.

Core behaviors:

- Staffs rehearse decision-making under time constraint.
- The design forces explicit tradeoff acknowledgement rather than deferral.
- Cross-functional integration is observable and assessable during execution.
- Decisions carry forward, and consequences compound across subsequent turns.

Website relevance:

- This is central to explaining the exercise design.
- It helps distinguish CADE from a knowledge test, classroom event, or simulation demo.
- It gives concrete language for what the capstone actually trains.

Potential section headline:

- "The training target was decision behavior, not doctrinal recall."

### Boundaries And Scope Discipline

CADE was not intended to:

- Certify doctrinal competency.
- Grade participants on a pass/fail basis.
- Replace high-fidelity simulation for every training use case.
- Provide a resettable environment where mistakes disappear.

Website relevance:

- Useful for credibility. Clear boundaries make the project feel mature and defensible.
- This can support a "What CADE Is / Is Not" section.
- It also protects against overclaiming AI capability.

Potential section headline:

- "Scope discipline kept the design honest."

### Training Mechanism

Four mechanisms produced the training effect:

- Six-phase sequence with fixed rhythm and enforced accountability.
- Adjudication tied to observable behavior, not controller judgment.
- Decisions carry forward and consequences compound across turns.
- Controllers enforce structure; they do not steer deliberation.

Website relevance:

- This is strong middle-page content because it explains how CADE works.
- It provides a clear system diagram candidate.
- It supports claims about assessability, repeatability, and facilitation.

Potential visual:

### Six-Phase Turn Sequence

Every CADE turn follows the same sequence:

1. Situation
2. Clarification
3. Deliberation
4. Decision
5. Brief to CDR
6. Adjudication

Website relevance:

- This should likely appear on the website as a compact process graphic.
- The key message is that fixed rhythm makes the exercise assessable and outcomes comparable across turns.

Potential section headline:

### AI Role Versus Human Design Authority

The briefing is clear that AI was the production engine, not the design authority.

Human design authority:

- Identified the training gap.
- Set behavioral markers as success criteria.
- Authored the six-phase sequence.
- Wrote adjudication logic.
- Set controller discipline standards.
- Observed live execution and decided what changed afterward.
- Made the Controller Package architecture decision.
- Set multilingual delivery standards.

AI role:

- Generated Ukraine-specific research.
- Drafted the OPORD shell.
- Drafted research, OPORD artifacts, turn content, and controller materials.
- Iterated revised Alpine artifacts to new specifications.

Website relevance:

- This is one of the most important credibility points for an AI skills showcase.
- It demonstrates judgment, governance, and workflow design rather than generic AI usage.
- It should appear in the site as a "What AI Did / What I Did" comparison.

Potential section headline:

- "AI made speed possible. Human design authority made speed usable."

### Scenario Anchor And Execution Reliability

Execution reliability came from these design decisions:

- Sentinel Shield served as the single source of truth governing all artifacts.
- Operational terms replaced vague framing.

- Formal decision checkpoints were added.
- Review cadence was fixed after every adjudication cycle.
- Controller boundaries were clarified before execution pressure exposed ambiguity.

Website relevance:

### Measurable Success Metrics

The PRD defines success through observable live or rehearsal behavior:

| Metric | Website-Relevant Threshold |
| --- | --- |
| Facilitator independence | Controller team executes a full 3-turn event within a 2-day window using only the Controller Package and no designer present |
| Decision pressure | Each turn produces a documented Option A / Option B commitment from every staff section before the decision window closes |
| Adjudication consistency | Two independent controllers reach the same outcome band at least 80% of the time when applying the model to the same observed behavior |
| Consequence persistence | Turn 1 resource, casualty, or timing state materially constrains at least one Turn 3 decision |
| AAR quality | Turn-level AARs capture reasoning, expected outcomes, coordination gaps, and next-turn adjustment |
| Multilingual usability | Mixed-language audiences complete decision windows without comprehension-related time extensions |
| Preparation overhead | A unit with no prior CADE exposure reaches execution-ready status using published fit criteria and checklists |

Website relevance:

- These metrics can become a "How CADE is measured" section.
- They are useful proof because they avoid vague claims like "successful" or "effective."
- Several metrics can become small stat cards once validated with execution evidence.

Potential section headline:

- "Success is measured in execution behavior, not document polish."

### Five Core Product Modules

The PRD defines five core modules:

| Module | Purpose |
| --- | --- |
| Source-governed scenario package | Holds scenario narrative, operational graphics, control measures, and annex support as coherent source truth |
| Turn engine | Defines the turn lifecycle, phase transitions, time boxes, and decision commitments |
| Adjudication model | Converts observable integration behavior into deterministic outcome bands |
| Controller delivery package | Consolidates execution logic, quick references, role aids, decision prompts, and support tools into one runtime artifact |
| Learning capture layer | Standardizes turn-level AARs, reflection prompts, and final synthesis without turning CADE into a lecture |

Website relevance:

- This should likely become the main structure for a "System Architecture" section.
- It is a clearer framework than the current project-card model.
- It separates source production, exercise execution, control, adjudication, and learning capture in a way that maps well to visual design.

Potential visual:

### Acceptance Criteria Worth Preserving

The PRD includes acceptance criteria that may become proof or design standards on the site:

- No downstream product may contradict source package content.
- A designer unfamiliar with the original design should be able to verify source coherence using the package alone.
- Time boxes must be enforceable in rehearsal without designer intervention.
- Transition signals between phases must be unambiguous to a first-time controller.
- No adjudication outcome should require controller inference beyond what is directly observable.
- A controller should locate any required reference within 60 seconds under time pressure.
- The Controller Package should support at least one full rehearsal without supplemental materials.
- AARs should stay inside the protected time box while capturing reasoning, expected outcome, coordination gaps, and next-turn adjustment.

Website relevance:

- These details can make a "quality standard" section feel concrete.
- The 60-second reference threshold is especially useful as a product-usability proof point.

- The test question is whether CADE reliably produces decision pressure, visible tradeoffs, credible adjudication, and learning capture under constrained conditions.

### Testing Thresholds And Evidence Baseline

Useful thresholds from the PRD:

- Adjudication consistency: two controllers reach the same outcome band in 8 of 10 representative behavior cases.
- Persistent consequence tracking: in a 3-turn rehearsal, at least one Turn 3 option must be unavailable or degraded because of a Turn 1 decision.
- Controller Package usability: a controller team with no prior CADE experience can locate required references within 60 seconds during timed rehearsal.

Evidence baseline from January 29-30, 2026:

- Fragmented controller artifacts caused navigation failures under pressure.
- Adjudication inconsistency undermined outcome credibility.
- AAR windows were compressed by turn overruns.

Resulting design responses:

- Controller Package consolidation.
- Deterministic outcome bands.
- Protected AAR time boxes.

Website relevance:

- This is a useful "what broke / what changed" story.
- It shows that CADE evolved from observed execution failures rather than abstract preferences.

Potential section headline:

- "Execution evidence drove the v3.0 design."

### Risk Controls

The PRD identifies risks and mitigations that can be converted into a credibility section:

| Risk | Design Control |
| --- | --- |
| Controller discipline degrades without designer present | Role boundary documentation and rehearsal requirement |
| Adjudication inconsistency | Deterministic outcome bands and calibration rehearsal |
| Language friction delays decision windows | Visual-forward materials, plain-language phrasing, pre-event translation review |
| Consequence tracking breaks down | State tracking sheet and multi-turn rehearsal requirement |
| Runbook regression | Controller Package is the sole authorized runtime artifact |
| Scenario drift over repeated adaptations | Source-governed package rule |

Website relevance:

- This material shows product maturity.
- It can help write a section around "Designed against known failure modes."

### Governance And Evolution

The PRD defines controlled evolution:

- CADE should be governed as a constrained-environment training framework for decision behavior and combined arms integration.
- It should not be positioned as a general-purpose exercise platform.
- Structural change to the five modules requires recurring operational evidence, materially better training research, or new stakeholder requirements.
- Version-to-version change rationale must be documented.
- Reverting from Controller Package-centered execution back to fragmented controller products should be treated as regression risk.

Website relevance:

- This supports credibility for technical and institutional audiences.
- It explains how the project can evolve without losing the training effect.

Potential section headline:

- "CADE changes only when evidence justifies changing the system."

### Additional Out-Of-Scope Clarity

New out-of-scope detail from the PRD:

- CADE is not a long-term progressive skill-building program for individual proficiency over weeks or months.
- CADE does not require one universal scenario across all adopters; scenario content may vary if canonical CADE design logic is preserved.
- CADE does not fully automate design judgment through AI.

Website relevance:

- These details add nuance to the earlier boundaries.
- The "scenario content may vary" point is important because it explains how CADE can be reusable without being scenario-locked.
